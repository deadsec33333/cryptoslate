Federal Debt

The Federal Debt is the total amount of money that the U.S. government owes to creditors, including public and private entities, due to borrowing to cover budget deficits.

Source: FRED, Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis

Legend: BTC

U.S. Department of the Treasury. Fiscal Service, Federal Debt: Total Public Debt [GFDEBTN], retrieved from FRED, Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis; https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/GFDEBTN, December 8, 2024.

What is Federal Debt?

Federal Debt, often referred to as national debt, represents the total amount of money the U.S. government owes to creditors. These creditors include domestic and foreign individuals, corporations, and governments that purchase U.S. Treasury bonds and other securities. When the government runs a budget deficit—spending more than it earns in revenue through taxes—it borrows money to cover the shortfall, adding to the Federal Debt.

The debt is broken into two main components:

  1. Public Debt – Debt held by external entities such as individuals, foreign governments, and corporations.
  2. Intragovernmental Holdings – Debt the government owes to itself, such as borrowing from Social Security or other trust funds.

A Brief History of Federal Debt

The concept of federal debt has been central to the United States’ financial system since its inception. Here’s an overview of how it has evolved:

  • 1789-1900: Early U.S. debt was accumulated during the American Revolutionary War. The country’s financial infrastructure was rudimentary, and significant debt was incurred in founding and expanding the nation. Notably, during times of war, such as the Civil War, debt levels spiked dramatically.
  • 20th Century: The federal debt began to increase more significantly during the 20th century, especially during World War I and World War II, as the government borrowed heavily to fund military efforts. After World War II, the debt-to-GDP ratio peaked at over 100%, only to decline during the prosperous post-war years.
  • 1980s: During the Reagan administration, the national debt increased substantially due to tax cuts, increased military spending, and the early stages of modern deficit spending.
  • 2008 Financial Crisis: The global financial crisis led to unprecedented borrowing, as the U.S. government initiated large-scale stimulus measures to stabilize the economy. The Federal Debt surged as a result, with new programs aimed at saving financial institutions, bailing out industries, and providing economic relief.
  • COVID-19 Pandemic (2020): The COVID-19 pandemic marked another sharp increase in federal borrowing, with massive stimulus packages enacted to support individuals, businesses, and the economy as a whole. Federal Debt skyrocketed during this period, approaching $30 trillion by the end of 2021.

The Mechanics of Federal Debt

When the U.S. government borrows money, it issues Treasury bonds, bills, and notes, which are sold to investors. These securities are essentially loans to the government, which must pay back the principal amount with interest over time. Treasury securities are considered among the safest investments in the world because the U.S. government has never defaulted on its debt.

However, carrying too much debt can lead to various challenges, such as rising interest costs, limited ability to invest in other areas, and potential inflationary pressures. Policymakers continually debate the optimal balance between borrowing for necessary investments and ensuring the long-term sustainability of the debt.

The Relationship Between Federal Debt and Bitcoin

In recent years, there has been growing interest in how federal debt and monetary policy influence the price of Bitcoin and other crypto assets. The correlation between federal debt levels and Bitcoin prices is not direct, but macroeconomic trends tied to government borrowing, inflation, and currency debasement can have ripple effects on digital assets like Bitcoin.

Here’s how federal debt influences the Bitcoin market:

  1. Inflation and Currency Devaluation: As the federal debt grows, there is often concern about the devaluation of the U.S. dollar. When governments borrow excessively, central banks sometimes respond by increasing the money supply, which can trigger inflation. As a hedge against inflation and a store of value, Bitcoin has often been viewed as a safe-haven asset, similar to gold. During periods of rising debt and inflation fears, investors have historically turned to Bitcoin as a way to protect their wealth from currency devaluation.
  2. Monetary Easing and Bitcoin Price: In response to growing federal debt and economic downturns, central banks, including the U.S. Federal Reserve, have employed quantitative easing (QE) and near-zero interest rates to stimulate the economy. This easy-money environment has benefited risk assets, including Bitcoin. As federal debt expands, central banks are more likely to keep interest rates low, increasing liquidity in financial markets. Some of this liquidity finds its way into Bitcoin, pushing up its price.
  3. Investor Sentiment and Risk Perception: The growing size of the U.S. Federal Debt has also led to debates about the sustainability of traditional fiat currency systems. As distrust in government-backed currencies rises, some investors have turned to decentralized assets like Bitcoin. Bitcoin’s finite supply of 21 million coins is in stark contrast to fiat currencies, which can be inflated at will, contributing to Bitcoin’s appeal as a hedge against systemic financial risks.
  4. Bitcoin as a Safe Haven: Bitcoin has also been viewed as an alternative to traditional financial systems that rely heavily on debt. As federal debt reaches new heights, the risk of economic instability grows, leading some investors to see Bitcoin as a store of value independent of government intervention. This has led to Bitcoin being dubbed “digital gold,” as it is increasingly used as a safeguard against the systemic risks posed by growing federal debt and potential future financial crises.

Federal Debt and the Crypto Landscape

As the U.S. Federal Debt continues to grow, its implications stretch far beyond traditional financial markets. Bitcoin’s rise as an alternative store of value is, in part, a response to concerns over excessive government borrowing and the sustainability of fiat currencies.

While Bitcoin’s price does not move directly in tandem with federal debt levels, the macroeconomic conditions influenced by rising debt, such as inflation, monetary easing, and investor risk sentiment, have often contributed to Bitcoin’s appeal as a hedge against economic uncertainty.

For investors in Bitcoin and the broader crypto market, understanding these macroeconomic factors can provide valuable insights into future price movements and the evolving role of decentralized assets in the global financial system.
